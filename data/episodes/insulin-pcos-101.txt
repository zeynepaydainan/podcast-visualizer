
Welcome back. Today we are going to unpack one of the most important and most misunderstood relationships in women's health: the link between insulin resistance and PCOS, polycystic ovary syndrome. If you have PCOS, or you love someone who does, this mechanism explains a huge amount of what you are experiencing. And the good news is that it is one of the most modifiable systems in the body.

Let's start with the mechanism, because once you understand it, the tools make sense.

Insulin is the hormone your pancreas releases when you eat, especially when you eat carbohydrates. Its job is to move glucose out of the bloodstream and into your cells for energy. In insulin resistance, the cells stop responding well to that signal. So the pancreas does the intuitive thing: it shouts louder. It releases more insulin to get the same job done. The result is a state called hyperinsulinemia, chronically high circulating insulin. This is the first concept to hold onto: the problem is not just high blood sugar, it is high insulin.

Here is where PCOS enters. The ovaries are sensitive to insulin. When insulin is chronically elevated, it signals the ovaries to produce more androgens, hormones like testosterone. Those elevated androgens drive many of the classic PCOS symptoms: irregular cycles, acne, unwanted hair growth, and difficulty with ovulation. So high insulin is not a side issue in PCOS. For many women it is closer to the engine.

And this is the part most people miss. It is a loop, not a one-way street. Insulin resistance raises androgens, and the resulting hormonal environment can further worsen insulin sensitivity. Round and round. By some estimates around 70 percent of women with PCOS have some degree of insulin resistance, and critically, this includes lean women. You do not have to be overweight to have this loop running. That is a key point: PCOS-related insulin resistance is a metabolic story, not a body-size story.

So what actually moves the needle? Let's talk tools, and notice that most of these are about adding something helpful rather than restricting.

Tool one, and if you take nothing else, take this: build muscle. Skeletal muscle is the largest glucose sink in your body. It is where most of the glucose from a meal gets disposed of. When you do resistance training, even two or three sessions a week, you increase both the amount of muscle and its insulin sensitivity. You are literally building more capacity to clear glucose. Resistance training is arguably the single highest-leverage intervention here.

Tool two: take a short walk after meals. Ten to fifteen minutes of easy walking after eating measurably blunts the post-meal glucose spike, because contracting muscles pull in glucose without needing much insulin. Low effort, real effect.

Tool three: meal sequencing. Eating your protein, fiber, and vegetables before the starchier part of a meal slows how quickly glucose enters the bloodstream, which lowers the insulin your body needs to release. Same food, gentler curve.

Tool four: prioritize protein and fiber generally. Both slow digestion and improve satiety, which supports steadier insulin levels through the day.

Tool five: protect your sleep. This one is underrated. Even a few nights of poor sleep can measurably reduce insulin sensitivity in healthy people. Sleep is not a luxury in this system, it is a lever.

Tool six, on the supplement side: inositol. Myo-inositol and D-chiro-inositol, often studied together, have a solid evidence base for improving insulin sensitivity and supporting ovulation in PCOS. This is one to raise with your doctor rather than self-prescribe, but the research is genuinely encouraging.

And that points to the last and most important tool: work with a clinician. Ask for the right labs. Discuss whether something like metformin fits your picture. The lifestyle tools and the medical tools are not rivals, they work best together.

So to bring it home. Insulin resistance and PCOS are locked in a feedback loop, driven by chronically high insulin pushing the ovaries toward excess androgens. The loop is real, it affects lean and higher-weight women alike, and it is remarkably responsive to how you move, eat, and sleep. Build muscle, walk after meals, sequence your food, guard your sleep, and partner with a good clinician.

That is the loop, and those are your levers. Thanks for listening.
