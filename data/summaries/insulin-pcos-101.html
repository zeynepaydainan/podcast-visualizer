Insulin Resistance & PCOS: The Unseen Link

Unraveling the hormonal connection and actionable strategies for women's health.

70%
of women with PCOS have insulin resistance

Insulin resistance is a foundational driver in Polycystic Ovary Syndrome (PCOS), where cells become less responsive to insulin, leading to chronically high insulin levels that directly stimulate excess androgen production in the ovaries.

1

Insulin Function

Insulin is a hormone released by the pancreas, primarily after eating carbohydrates, to move glucose from the bloodstream into cells for energy.

2

Insulin Resistance

This occurs when cells become less responsive to insulin's signal, causing the pancreas to produce more insulin to achieve the same effect of clearing glucose.

3

Hyperinsulinemia

This is a state of chronically high levels of circulating insulin in the bloodstream, often resulting from insulin resistance.

4

Insulin-Androgen Link in PCOS

Chronically elevated insulin directly stimulates the ovaries to produce excessive amounts of androgens (like testosterone), which then drive many classic PCOS symptoms.

5

Insulin Resistance-PCOS Feedback Loop

Insulin resistance leads to higher androgen production, and this elevated androgen environment can, in turn, further worsen insulin sensitivity, creating a self-perpetuating cycle.

6

Muscle Building for Glucose Disposal

Building skeletal muscle through resistance training increases the body's capacity to absorb glucose from meals and improves overall insulin sensitivity.

7

Post-meal Walking for Glucose Control

Taking a short walk after eating causes muscles to contract and pull glucose from the bloodstream, thereby blunting post-meal glucose spikes with less need for insulin.

8

Meal Sequencing for Insulin Response

Eating protein, fiber, and vegetables before starches slows down glucose entry into the bloodstream, which reduces the amount of insulin the body needs to release.

9

Protein and Fiber for Insulin Stability

Prioritizing protein and fiber in the diet slows digestion and promotes satiety, helping to maintain steadier insulin levels throughout the day.

10

Sleep's Impact on Insulin Sensitivity

Adequate sleep is crucial because even a few nights of poor sleep can significantly reduce the body's sensitivity to insulin.

11

Inositol for Insulin Sensitivity in PCOS

Supplements like myo-inositol and D-chiro-inositol have been shown to improve insulin sensitivity and support healthy ovulation in women with PCOS.

Actionable Tips

💪

Build muscle through resistance training

Skeletal muscle is the body's largest glucose sink. Increasing muscle and its insulin sensitivity builds more capacity to clear glucose, making it a high-leverage intervention for insulin resistance.

🚶‍♀️

Take a short walk after meals

Contracting muscles pull in glucose without needing much insulin, which measurably blunts the post-meal glucose spike.

🍽️

Practice meal sequencing

This method slows how quickly glucose enters the bloodstream, reducing the amount of insulin your body needs to release for the same food.

🥗

Prioritize protein and fiber

Both protein and fiber slow digestion and improve satiety, which helps support steadier insulin levels throughout the day.

😴

Protect your sleep

Even a few nights of poor sleep can measurably reduce insulin sensitivity, making good sleep a crucial lever in this system.

💊

Discuss inositol with your doctor

These supplements have a solid evidence base for improving insulin sensitivity and supporting ovulation in PCOS.

👩‍⚕️

Work with a clinician

A clinician can help you get the right lab tests, discuss medical interventions like metformin, and ensure lifestyle and medical tools work best together.
